Creativity in human-computer interaction

We were all born with creative genes. Without knowing much about the world, we were once curious, advanturous, and full of questions. But since when did these fade away for some of us? Recent advances in machine learning and computer vision enhance the interests in designing technology that fosteres human creativity. However, the lies in the fact that our understanding for creativity remains so limited. Therefore, my research program intends to build theoretical groundwork for human creativity in computer-mediated environment and to offer implications for future applications accordingly. Based on theories across the fields of communication, information sciences, human development, and cognitive sciences, my research projects investigate the relationship between media affordances and exploratory behaviors . Specifically, they are designed to answer one or more of the three key questions below through lab-based experiments, qualitative studies, and/or computational social sciences approaches:

  • What is creativity?
  • How is creativity formed?
  • How can creativity be further developed?

Creativity: What is it?

By observing and inquiring how users identity creative content online, the first phase of my research program studies how individuals acquires new information across computer-mediated platforms. This stage of the research informs how curated experiences in individuals’ digital life shape their concept toward creativity. Up to date, research in creativity relies on definitions elaborated by scholars and domain experts. However, to design user-centered applications that augment creativity, there is the need to understand what the greater population consider as creativity. To attempt this objective, I conduct a series of focus groups and a longitudinal study , tracking and analyzing users' visit and view data on a mock content website.

What does it mean to be creative? Through a series of 14 focus groups, I asked participants to find examples of creative content and discuss why they were creative (or not).

Toward novel experiences:
How is creativity formed?

The second part of my research program focuses on how individuals leverage media affordances to generate creative products in novel experiences, including the encounter of a novel environment, a novel self, and a novel partner. In a preliminary study, Surreal or Real , I investigated how participants’ idea generation outcomes were influenced by being present in mixed (MR) vs. virtual reality (VR). In the second project, Embodied Genius , I examined whether participants demonstrated behavioral differences when embodying in novel avatars of innovative characters (e.g., Albert Einstein). The third project, Idea Bot, assesses the effect of interacting with a novel partner on a brainstorming task. Through a series of three-staged online experiments, I tested whether the perceived identity and conversational style of an artificial chatbot influence participants’ creative production.

In addition to conducting experiments, I also take part in several field studies, investigating how users explore and harness the media affordances of VR for academic conferences (an observation and interview study based on participants experiences in the 2020 IEEE VR Conference) and VR for remote learning (a cross-institution longitudinal study investigating students learning outcomes in VR courses throughout the 2020-2021 academic year).

Embodied Geniuses

Self-efficacy serves a critical role in one's creative capacity. Based on the classic proteus effect in VR research, I investigate whether embodying in avatars of various creative geniuses can influence the way individuals think and behave in an idea generation tasks.

Learning in the new "normal" time

The worldwide COVID-19 pandemic has changed our life forever. How do students adapt to the new "normality"? In a year-long study, we investigate how VR is used as a platform for remote education.

Into the Unknown:
Fostering creativity in computer-mediated environment

How can creativity be further developed? Informed by the first two phases of my research program, "what is creativity?" and "how is creativity formed?", the final stage is to design prototypes which augment team creativity by introducing novel experiences that users cannot attain in their real life. Enlightened by a common technique – drawing with your undominated hand – that has long been used in free-hand drawing classes for inspiration, I have already been looking into a number of design ideas in our research lab include harnessing homuncular flexibility (e.g., making use of an extra arm, controlling hand movements with legs), reversing time perception in response to one’s movement (e.g., presenting slower motion and scene changes when participants move faster), embodying in non-human objects (e.g., embodying in a cube versus a humanoid avatar), shifting points-of-view (e.g., attaining a first-person versus third-person perspectives), and leveraging the fourth dimension (e.g., creating experiences similar to the 4D VR games and SuperHyperCube). By manipulating the tracked and rendered motions, these novel experiences can be created in VR. I am interested in investigating how participants could learn, explore, and adapt to these unexperienced features and to perform tasks and organize information more efficiently, and ultimately, how these experiencse can influence individuals' creativity, not only in the artificial world but also in the reality later on.
